Convergence — the canonical gate

A decision, in writing, about whether a run of agents has produced something worth sealing — and the machinery that renders it impossible to skip.

Stable since 3.0 API Reference Tutorial Last revised · 2026-08-12 Contributors · 7 Cited in 14 runbooks

Every run the foundry produces ends with a written verdict. The convergence gate is the one you will meet first and live with longest; it is the canonical shape against which every other gate is measured, and in many teams, the only gate that ever needs to be customised.

Before going further: a gate is not a check. Checks are cheap things an agent does to itself — a lint, a schema validation, a self-critique. A gate is a separate verdict, written by a separate agent, that refuses to acknowledge the first one. When the two agents disagree, a third is seated; when the third cannot settle it, the run is handed to a human. This shape — two minds, then three, then yours — is the foundry in miniature, and convergence is the gate that formalises it.

§ 1The shape of a convergence gate

A convergence gate takes three inputs: the artefact produced by the run (anything from a sentence to a pull request), the criteria written into the role manifest, and the history of prior iterations on this particular artefact. It emits one of four verdicts, and nothing else.

Convergence, n.
A written statement, by an agent not party to the production of an artefact, that the artefact is indistinguishable in its salient properties from what a second, independent agent would have produced. Short form: two strangers, same answer.
Verdict, n.
One of pass, iterate, fail, or defer; written in prose, with a one-paragraph justification and, where applicable, a bounded list of required changes. See § 2.

Every verdict is prose, and every verdict is signed. There is no numerical confidence, no probability, no heat-map — the foundry treats those as performance, not meaning. A convergence gate that cannot be written in one paragraph is a gate that is not yet understood.

“Two strangers, same answer. Everything else is ornament.” Praxis Foundry handbook, 2024

§ 2The four verdicts, in order of appetite

The verdicts are not scores, they are commitments about what happens next. Reading them in order:

  1. Pass. The verifier has read the artefact, compared it to the criteria, and will put their name to it. The run is sealed; the ledger updates; downstream seals cascade. Nothing further happens automatically.
  2. Iterate. The verifier can name, in one paragraph, what would need to change for a pass. The run is returned to the editing agent with the paragraph attached. Iteration is budgeted (see Chapter V · soft-caps); unbounded iteration becomes fail.
  3. Fail. The artefact is not of the kind the criteria describe. This is not a gentler form of iterate; it is a refusal to iterate. A fail verdict always names the misalignment and stops the run cold.
  4. Defer. The verifier declines to issue a verdict — typically because the criteria are silent on the question at hand. A human is summoned. Defer is the only verdict that tolerates ambiguity; iterate and fail must be confident.

§ 3Authoring a verdict, by hand

The fastest way to understand the gate is to write a verdict yourself, for a run that has already happened. Open any run in the ledger, press ⌥ V, and the verdict composer opens with the artefact and the criteria side by side. The composer enforces four fields:

# A convergence verdict is plain YAML, signed by its author.
verdict:   pass            # one of: pass | iterate | fail | defer
criterion: §4.2-tariff     # which rule in the manifest you applied
paragraph: |
  I reconciled each line item against the published tariff for the
  shipment date. The six flagged rows fall outside §4.2 in the
  specific ways noted in CLM-10412 .. CLM-10602. All other rows
  are internally consistent. I recommend PASS on the batch and a
  follow-up for the carrier on the six.
author:    verifier-03     # the seat that issued the verdict
sealed_at: 2026-08-14T14:22:51Z

The composer will refuse to submit a verdict whose paragraph is shorter than two full sentences, whose author is the same seat that produced the artefact, or whose criterion is not present in the manifest. These are not style suggestions — they are the minimum conditions under which a verdict counts as a verdict at all.

!
If the manifest is silent on the question, defer.
Agents that guess past their criteria are a form of drift. See Chapter VI, The principle of least scope.

§ 4What the gate refuses to do

A convergence gate does not rank, does not score, and does not “mostly agree.” It does not average verdicts from an ensemble and call that a verdict. It does not inherit trust from prior runs — a verifier that passed a hundred runs in a row is not thereby licensed to pass the hundred-and-first without reading it. The gate is written in such a way that these failure modes are syntactically unavailable, not merely discouraged.

This is the single design decision that makes the foundry boring, and it is the decision most teams want to undo first. Resist. A noisy, readable gate is cheaper, over a year, than a clever one that drifts. If you find yourself wanting a numerical threshold, what you want is probably a check — promote it to the editor, not the verifier.

The difference between an audit and a theatre is that an audit can say no, and a theatre cannot. Ida Leclerc · a5c field notes, §12

§ 5Replay & the seal

Every convergence verdict is sealed with a cinnabar mark, which in practice is a signed commit pointing at the artefact’s content hash and the verifier’s role manifest at the moment of verdict. Unsealing is possible but expensive: the run moves to the replay worktree, the verdict is retracted in the ledger (with a visible strike-through rather than a delete), and any downstream seals that depended on it are marked provisional until they are re-run.

The replay worktree is not a rollback — the foundry does not believe in rollback. It is a parallel workspace in which the original verdict is preserved, a new verdict is rendered, and the two are placed side by side in the ledger. Auditors read both. See Chapter IV, § 3 for the full walk-through, including the audit trail that survives any future disagreement about what was decided and by whom.

Marginal gloss

Cinnabar seal
The vermilion mark a verifier affixes to a sealed verdict. Originally a wet stamp; now a signed content hash. Not revocable without a replay.
Role manifest
A plain-text description of an agent’s seat, tools, and criteria. The gate is a subset of the manifest.
Replay worktree
A parallel workspace in which a retracted verdict is re-rendered without destroying the original. See also rollback, refusal of.
